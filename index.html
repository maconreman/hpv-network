HPV Discourse Study
Title

Networks of Influence

Exploring Online Human Papillomavirus (HPV) Vaccine Discussions through Network Analysis and Topic Modeling.

Background

Background

HPV is a sexually transmitted infection, and persistent infection can lead to cervical cancer. Cervical cancer rates are highest in low- and middle-income countries, largely because of limited vaccine access, weak screening infrastructure, and social and economic barriers.

In the Philippines, the Department of Health added HPV vaccination to the national immunization program in 2015. Even so, by 2023 only about 11.67% of females had completed the full vaccine series. To close that gap, DOH partnered with the Department of Education on a school-based program vaccinating female students aged 9–10 in public schools.

Access alone doesn't guarantee uptake. Vaccine hesitancy is shaped by four overlapping factors: historical, political, and socio-cultural context; public health and vaccine policy; recommendations from health professionals; and communication and media. In the Philippines specifically, the Dengvaxia controversy left a lasting mark on how Filipinos view vaccines generally.

Social media adds another layer. It gives far more people a platform to discuss vaccines, but with little built-in ability to separate accurate information from misinformation. That gap is what motivates looking closely at how HPV is actually being discussed on Facebook and TikTok.

Research Problem

Research Problem

This study looks at HPV-related discourse on Facebook and TikTok, specifically:

  1. To identify the key accounts and actors driving HPV-related conversation on each platform.
  2. To understand what public health knowledge and gaps show up in that conversation.
  3. To recommend health communication strategies that respond to what's actually being said.

The main goal is to know who the influential voices are and which platforms carry the most relevant discussion, which helps identify who to engage, where misinformation needs to be countered, and how to design messaging that actually reaches people where they already are.

Methods

Methods

Data collection. Facebook and TikTok posts mentioning HPV were collected from January 2020 through July 2024, using over 15 search keywords (e.g. "hpv," "hpv vaccine," "cervical cancer vaccine," "gardasil," "pap smear"). Facebook data came from CrowdTangle; TikTok data came from Apify, with a supplemental TikTok pull in February 2025 to address a low initial post count.

Preprocessing. Duplicate and near-duplicate posts were removed, along with content that wasn't actually about HPV, to keep the dataset focused on vaccine safety, efficacy, campaigns, and personal experience content.

Network construction. Each platform's network was built differently, matching how the platform actually works: on Facebook, an edge runs from the account that shared a piece of content to the account that received it. On TikTok, an edge connects two accounts that used the same HPV-related hashtag. Community detection used the Louvain algorithm.

Topic modeling. The original study used BERTopic (with class-based TF-IDF term weighting) in multilingual mode, since posts appear in both English and Filipino.
